Project: The Space-Garden Habitat (teachers.md)
1. The Philosophy: "Mission Control, Not a Hobby"
Most school gardens fail because of "dry" budgets or lack of engagement. We treat the garden as a Space Habitat. If the sensors fail or the "Comms" (WiFi) go down, the mission is at risk. This engages the "IT nerds" and the "Shop kids" simultaneously.
2. The Budget-Hardware (The "Harbor Freight" Tier)
Don't buy expensive "educational" kits. Use rugged, industrial alternatives:
The Thermal Shield: Fiberglass Welding Blankets. Use these to wrap hydroponic reservoirs or create shade. They insulate against the desert heat and look like NASA spacecraft insulation.
The Structure: Fiberglass Wire-Running Rods. Use these as flexible, non-corroding stakes for sensors or plant supports.
The Power: Portable Power Stations & Solar Panels. Teaches the physics of "Starlight Energy" (Solar) and battery cycles.
3. The Digital Backbone (The "IT Engine Room")
We use the "BOFH" (Bastard Operator From Hell) school of learning.
The Local Mesh: Use small WiFi "Dots" (ESP32/Raspberry Pi) to form a network that doesn't rely on the school's unreliable ISP.
The Interface: Home Assistant. It is the "Mission Control" dashboard.
The Protocol: IRC (Internet Relay Chat). It’s lightweight and survives outages. Students must learn to "command" the garden via text-based protocols.
The Language: Rust. We use Rust because it’s "Space-Grade." It prevents the students from writing code that crashes the life-support systems (the pumps).
4. The Future-Proofing: Local AI
By building this local network, you are preparing the school for its own Local LLM.
Because the data stays local (via Home Assistant and MQTT), you can eventually plug in a local AI to "talk" to the garden.
"McDonald's at Home" logic: We don't need a $100/month cloud AI subscription if we build the "compute habitat" in the shade of our own garden.
5. Teacher's "Cheat Sheet"
Step 1: Define the "Egress" (What data leaves the garden?).
Step 2: Watch the MOTD (Message of the Day). If the student's code makes the CPU run hot, the MOTD will tell them they are "wasting oxygen" (power).
Step 3: Let them break it. The best lessons happen when the "Rogers" (ISP) fails and the students realize their garden is the only thing still online.
